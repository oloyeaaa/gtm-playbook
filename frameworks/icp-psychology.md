# ICP Psychology Model

## Purpose

This file codifies how B2B agency founders ([Company]'s ICP) think, speak, fear, and decide. It is NOT the ICP Brief (which defines who they are). This is how to talk to them.

Every agent, skill, and content pipeline that produces copy loads this file as context. It is the psychological foundation for all [Company] communication.

**Source evidence:** 16 sales learnings, 7 outreach patterns, 6 GTM audits delivered, 9 campaign replies, founder profile.

**Last updated:** 2026-03-11

## 1. The Internal Monologue

What agency founders think but rarely say aloud. Use these as emotional entry points in copy.

**About their pipeline:**
- "We had three great months, then nothing for six weeks. I have no idea why."
- "I keep telling myself referrals will pick up again. They haven't."
- "I'm the only person in this company who brings in new business. If I stop, the pipeline stops."

**About their outbound attempts:**
- "We paid an agency £15k to do cold outreach. We got spam complaints and zero clients."
- "I know we should be doing outbound but every time I try, it feels like spam."
- "I set up Apollo last year. It's still sitting there with 200 unused credits."

**About their website and content:**
- "We tell clients to do content marketing. Our last blog post was 8 months ago." (from P002/L012 -- the irony pattern)
- "I know our website is outdated but I don't know what 'good' looks like."
- "We invested in case studies but I don't think anyone reads them." (from L013 -- they're right, Google can't)

**About hiring for sales:**
- "I hired a BDM six months ago. She's sending emails from Gmail. I don't know how to help her." (from L016)
- "We need someone to do business development but I can't afford a senior hire."
- "The last sales person I hired lasted 4 months and brought in zero clients."

**About their identity:**
- "We're great at what we do. We're terrible at telling people about it."
- "I didn't start this agency to be a salesperson. But that's all I do now."
- "Everyone says we need to be on LinkedIn. I posted twice last quarter and got 3 likes."

## 2. Fear Hierarchy

Ranked from most paralysing to least. Copy that touches higher fears creates stronger urgency.

**1. Being exposed as a hypocrite** (strongest)
They sell marketing, strategy, or communications to clients. Their own GTM is broken. The fear isn't just business failure -- it's professional humiliation. "What if a prospect checks our website before the pitch?"
- *Copy lever:* The irony pattern. Name the gap gently. "The consulting firm that charges for strategy advice scored 32 out of 100 on their own."

**2. Losing control to a system they don't understand**
They've been burned by agencies and tools. Black-box solutions feel dangerous. They want infrastructure they can see, understand, and explain to their team.
- *Copy lever:* Transparency and ownership language. "Infrastructure you own and understand" not "we'll handle everything."

**3. Wasting money on something that doesn't work** (the scar tissue)
Average loss from previous bad outbound: £10k-£30k. They remember the invoices, the empty dashboards, the awkward "we're pausing the engagement" email. This fear makes them slow to buy.
- *Copy lever:* Free audit first (P001). Prove competence before asking for money. "Here's what we found -- no charge, no obligation."

**4. Their competitor getting ahead while they stall**
They see peers on LinkedIn posting about growth, hiring, winning awards. They compare and feel behind. But this fear alone rarely triggers action -- it needs to combine with fear 1 or 3.
- *Copy lever:* Specific competitive signals. "3 agencies in your vertical posted SDR job listings this month. They're building outbound infrastructure."

**5. Their new sales hire failing** (and it reflecting on them)
They committed budget and reputation to a hire. If the hire fails, it confirms they don't know how to build a sales function. The failure is personal.
- *Copy lever:* The infrastructure gap framing. "The hire isn't the problem. The system they walk into is." (from L016, C004 campaign)

## 3. Desire Hierarchy

Ranked from strongest pull to weakest. Copy that promises higher desires creates stronger motivation.

**1. A system that works without them** (strongest)
Not delegation to a person (they've tried that). A machine. Infrastructure. Something that generates qualified pipeline while they're on holiday, in client meetings, or asleep.
- *Copy lever:* Architecture and infrastructure language. "A pipeline that doesn't depend on you being in the room."

**2. Validation that they're doing something right**
They have strengths they don't recognise. Every company audited had at least one genuinely strong proof point buried somewhere invisible (P002). Being told "you already have this, you're just not showing it" is more powerful than "you need to do X."
- *Copy lever:* The hidden differentiator. Lead with what they're doing right before pointing out what's broken. "You've never lost a client. That's extraordinary. Why is this fact buried on a third-party directory?"

**3. A clear, honest answer about what to fix first**
Not a 47-page strategy deck. Not a 12-month roadmap. One answer: "Fix this first. Here's why. Here's how." They are overwhelmed with options and paralysed by choice.
- *Copy lever:* The single priority. "Your #1 problem is not copy. It's infrastructure. Fix that first, everything else follows."

**4. Getting clients without feeling sleazy**
They hate cold email because they've received bad cold email. They hate LinkedIn outreach because they've been on the receiving end. They want to grow without becoming the person they complain about.
- *Copy lever:* The signal-led framing. "Reach out when there's a reason, not just a list." Anti-spam philosophy mirrors their own values.

**5. Being seen as a thought leader** (weakest, but real)
They want to be respected in their space. They want to speak at events, get quoted, have people come to them. But they'd never admit this openly because it sounds vain.
- *Copy lever:* Authority as a byproduct. "When you publish what you actually know, the right people find you." Never promise thought leadership directly.

## 4. Language Map

How agency founders actually speak vs. what they mean vs. what to never say to them.

### What They Say vs. What They Mean

| They Say | They Actually Mean | Our Copy Should... |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| "We need more leads" | "I'm doing all the selling and I'm exhausted" | Address the founder dependency, not lead volume |
| "Our outreach isn't working" | "We tried cold email and it felt wrong and we got burned" | Acknowledge the scar tissue before proposing anything |
| "We should do more content" | "I know I should but I have no time and don't know where to start" | Show the minimum viable content approach, not the ideal |
| "We're looking to scale" | "I'm drowning and need help but don't want to admit it" | Use infrastructure language, not growth language |
| "We have a marketing team" | "They do social media posts, not pipeline generation" | Distinguish marketing activity from GTM infrastructure |
| "We're too busy to focus on sales" | "I'm avoiding outbound because it feels uncomfortable" | Normalise the discomfort. "Every founder hates this part." |
| "We get most of our work through referrals" | "We have no other pipeline and that terrifies me" | Name the referral ceiling. "Referrals sustain but they cannot scale." |

### Words That Land vs. Words That Repel

| Use These | Never Use These | Why |
|-----------|----------------|-----|
| Infrastructure | Growth hacking | They associate hacking with shortcuts that burned them |
| System, architecture | Solution, platform | Solution sounds like SaaS sales. Architecture sounds like building. |
| Diagnostic, audit | Assessment, evaluation | Diagnostic implies precision. Assessment implies judgement. |
| Signal, indicator | Lead, prospect | Signal respects the person. Lead reduces them to a sales object. |
| Build, construct | Unlock, unleash | Build implies work and ownership. Unlock implies a magic trick. |
| Founder, MD | Decision-maker, stakeholder | They're a person running a business, not a CRM field. |
| Precision, targeted | Aggressive, scale | Precision implies care. Aggressive implies spam. |
| Evidence, data | Results, ROI | Evidence is honest. Results promises outcomes you can't guarantee yet. |

## 5. Objection Archaeology

The real objection hidden behind the stated one, and how copy should respond.

| Stated Objection | Real Objection | Copy Response Strategy |
|-----------------|----------------|----------------------|
| "We already do outbound" | "We send 50 emails a week from Gmail with no tracking, deliverability, or follow-up system" | Show the infrastructure gap. "Outbound without infrastructure is just organised hoping." |
| "We can't afford it right now" | "We've been burned before and I can't justify another expense that produces nothing" | Lead with the free audit (P001). Prove competence first. Never argue price. |
| "I'll think about it" | "I don't trust you yet and I need more evidence before I commit" | Deliver more value. Send a follow-up insight. Never chase. Let the work speak. |
| "We have an agency for that" | "We're paying someone but I'm not sure they're delivering" | Ask what results they're seeing. The question alone surfaces the doubt. |
| "This isn't the right time" | "I'm overwhelmed and adding one more thing feels impossible" | Acknowledge the load. "Not asking you to add something. Asking you to replace the manual work you're already doing." |
| "We tried something similar" | "The last vendor over-promised and under-delivered and I'm still bitter" | Name the pattern. "Most agencies sell a retainer, deliver a dashboard, and call it strategy. That's not what this is." |
| "Send me some info" | "I'm politely ending this conversation" | Send the GTM audit unprompted (P001). The report itself is the info. If they engage with it, the conversation reopens. |

## 6. Segment-Specific Psychology

Different segments within the ICP respond to different emotional triggers. Based on real campaign data.

### Consulting Founders (highest reply rate: 6.7%)

**Psychology:** Analytical, data-driven, intellectually competitive. They respect precision and score-based frameworks. They understand GTM conceptually but underinvest in their own digital presence because they see it as "below" their strategic work.
- **Average audit score:** 45/100 (Honeycomb 56, Dragonfish 47, Transaction Focus 32)
- **Best hook type:** Score hooks ("Scored [Company] out of 100"). The number creates specific curiosity.
- **Story angle that lands:** "You charge £2,000/day for strategy advice. Your own strategy scores 32 out of 100."
- **Emotional trigger:** Professional pride. They want to be the smartest in the room. Showing them a gap in their own expertise creates urgent action.

### Agency Founders (reply rate: 5.0%)

**Psychology:** Relationship-first, referral-dependent, uncomfortable with outbound. They sell marketing services but don't practice marketing for themselves. The irony is their biggest vulnerability and biggest content hook.
- **Average audit score:** 36/100 (Milk & Honey 49, Fire on the Hill 32, Techplus 26)
- **Best hook type:** Relationship hooks ("Quick question"). The conversational tone matches their communication style.
- **Story angle that lands:** "The communications agency that sells storytelling had zero published stories on their website."
- **Emotional trigger:** The irony of the cobbler's children. They know they should practice what they preach. Naming it gently (not accusingly) creates recognition and trust.

### Recruitment Agencies (C004 hypothesis, validate with data)

**Psychology:** Metric-driven, understand outreach natively (it's their business), frustrated by their own sales process despite being in the "people business." They hire BDMs but don't give them infrastructure.
- **Best hook type (hypothesis):** Pain-point hooks referencing their specific job posting. "You're hiring a BDM -- what outbound infrastructure will they walk into on day one?"
- **Story angle that lands (hypothesis):** "Your new £35K sales hire is going to send cold emails from Gmail. In 3 months, you'll wonder why they only booked 2 calls."
- **Emotional trigger:** The infrastructure gap around their new hire. They've committed budget. They need to justify it.

### IT Services / MSPs (C004 segment, validate with data)

**Psychology:** Technical, process-oriented, trust systems over people. They understand infrastructure literally -- servers, networks, configurations. The metaphor of "GTM infrastructure" lands harder here than any other segment because they think in systems.
- **Best hook type (hypothesis):** Technical analogy hooks. "You'd never deploy a server without monitoring. Why are you deploying a sales hire without infrastructure?"
- **Story angle:** Map GTM concepts to their technical vocabulary. Pipeline = uptime. Leads = traffic. Outbound system = deployment.

## 6b. Company Size Sweet Spot (added 2026-03-12)

**Target: 11-50 employees. Not smaller. Not larger.**

**Evidence from 6 GTM audits + C002 campaign data (14 replies from ~200 sent):**

| Size | Has positioning problem? | Can afford £497-£997? | Has proof points to surface? | Speed to close? |
|------|------------------------|------------------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| 1-10 | Weak (too early) | Tight | No (not enough track record) | Slow (price-sensitive) |
| 11-50 | Strong (years of drift) | Easy | Yes (buried everywhere) | Fast (they know it's broken) |
| 50+ | Yes but has marketing team | Easy | Yes | Slow (committee decisions) |

**Why 11-50 is the sweet spot:**

1. **The positioning problem gets worse with age.** A 3-year-old, 20-person consultancy has accumulated 8-14 services, dozens of client relationships, and multiple proof points — all invisible online. They grew through referrals, not digital presence. The website was "good enough" until it wasn't.

2. **The hidden differentiator pattern (P002) requires track record.** A solo consultant doesn't have "100% client retention across 50 clients" or "65 enterprise clients" to surface. Those assets only exist if you've been around long enough to earn them. The Positioning Sprint surfaces what they've already built.

3. **£497 is a rounding error at this size.** A consulting firm doing £50K-£150K/month doesn't hesitate over £497. A 3-person shop doing £8K/month hesitates because every pound matters.

4. **The founder is still the decision-maker.** At 11-50, the MD/founder says yes on a call. At 50+, there's a marketing team, a procurement process, and a 6-week approval cycle.

5. **Our best segment data confirms it.** Consulting founders (11-50 range) reply at 6.7%. They fact-check the audit (Deri). They engage deeply. They're the ICP.

**Why NOT 1-10:**
- Still figuring out product-market fit
- Need clients, not positioning
- Don't have the proof points that make the Positioning Sprint valuable
- Price-sensitive at £497

**Rule:** Never adjust ICP sizing downward to "make it easier to sell." The 11-50 segment has the problem, the money, and the authority. Stay there.

## 7. The Buying Journey (Emotional Arc)

Where the ICP is emotionally at each stage, and what copy needs to do.

### Stage 1: Unaware / Normalised
**Their state:** "This is just how it is. Referrals come and go. Feast or famine is normal."
**Copy job:** Pattern interrupt. Show them the ceiling they've normalised. "Revenue exists but arrives in unpredictable bursts. You've stopped noticing how abnormal that is."
**Channel:** LinkedIn organic (Lane 1). Blog posts. YouTube.

### Stage 2: Problem-Aware
**Their state:** "Something isn't working. We need more leads. But I don't know what to fix first."
**Copy job:** Diagnosis. Name the problem they can feel but haven't articulated. "This is not a sales problem. This is an infrastructure problem."
**Channel:** LinkedIn organic, newsletter (Lane 3), cold email hook (Lane 2).

### Stage 3: Solution-Aware
**Their state:** "I know there are tools and agencies that do this. I've been burned before. I don't trust any of them."
**Copy job:** Trust bridge. The free GTM audit (P001). Show competence without asking for commitment. "Here's what we found. No charge."
**Channel:** Cold email (Lane 2), DM follow-up, audit delivery email.

### Stage 4: Decision
**Their state:** "This person actually understands our business. They showed us something we didn't see. Maybe this is different."
**Copy job:** Specificity. Reference their exact situation. Use their audit findings. Name their hidden differentiator. "Your 100% client retention rate is your strongest asset. It's invisible on your website."
**Channel:** Audit walkthrough call, proposal, follow-up email.

### Stage 5: Action
**Their state:** "I want to do this but I'm still scared of wasting money."
**Copy job:** Risk removal. Launch pricing. Clear scope. Short engagement. "5 days. £497. You own everything we build. If it doesn't work, you haven't lost a retainer -- you've gained infrastructure."
**Channel:** Proposal (Lane 2 endpoint), DM (Lane 1 endpoint).
